Indian Festivals

Festivals are the oldest rituals and traditions our countrymen follow to pay tribute to the almighty gods and goddesses

About Indian Festivals

Sankrati Festival

Sankranti is a Hindu harvest festival celebrated across India marking the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn, symbolizing new beginnings and the end of the winter solstice.

Dewali Festival

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is a significant Hindu festival celebrated with lamps, fireworks, and the exchange of sweets, symbolizing the triumph of light over darkness and good over evil.

Dussehra Festival

Dussehra, also known as Vijayadashami, is a Hindu festival that commemorates the victory of good over evil, particularly the triumph of Lord Rama over the demon king Ravana, symbolized by the burning of effigies and cultural performances.

Ramzan Festival

Ramzan, also known as Ramadan, is an Islamic holy month marked by fasting from dawn to sunset, devoted to prayer, reflection, and community, culminating in the celebration of Eid al-Fitr.

Makara Sankranti Festival

About Sankranti

Sankranti, a Hindu festival, celebrates the transition of the sun into Makara (Capricorn). It marks the end of the winter solstice and the onset of longer days. Observed in various parts of India, it's known by different names such as Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Makar Sankranti in North India, and Magh Bihu in Assam. People rejoice by flying kites, preparing traditional sweets like tilgul (sesame and jaggery), and exchanging greetings and gifts. Devotees take holy dips in rivers like the Ganges, praying for prosperity and purifying their sins. It signifies the harvest festival, honoring the land's bounty and expressing gratitude to nature for a successful crop yield. Sankranti holds cultural significance, symbolizing new beginnings, unity among communities, and the triumph of good over evil.
